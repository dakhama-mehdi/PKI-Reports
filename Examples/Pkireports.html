Weak signature algorithms in CSR Low
Some Certificate Signing Requests (CSR) were submitted using weak cryptographic algorithms such as SHA1 or MD5.
These algorithms are considered cryptographically broken and susceptible to collision attacks.

🚨 **Risk**: An attacker could craft two different CSRs that produce the same hash (collision), leading to potential impersonation or certificate forgery.

🎯 **Attack Scenario**: If the CA accepts such a CSR, it may sign a malicious certificate that appears legitimate, undermining trust in your PKI.

✅ **Recommendation**: Reject any CSR using SHA1, MD5, or RSA keys smaller than 2048 bits.
Ensure your enrollment policies or CAPolicy.inf enforce secure minimum requirements for key size and hash algorithm.

Template owned by non-privileged account High
One or more certificate templates are currently owned by unprivileged or user accounts that should not hold sensitive configuration control.

🚨 **Risk**: The owner of a template has full control over its settings, including access control lists (ACLs), issuance properties, and auto-enrollment settings.
An attacker compromising a low-privileged user could modify the template to enable unauthorized enrollment or impersonation.

🎯 **Attack Scenario**: An attacker takes ownership of a template, enables ENROLLEE_SUPPLIES_SUBJECT and Client Authentication, then enrolls a certificate with a fake subject (e.g. Domain Admin). This certificate can then be used for pass-the-cert attacks or authentication bypass (ESC1 / ESC2).

✅ **Recommendation**: Ensure that all certificate templates are owned by secure PKI administrators or dedicated service accounts.
You can use certtmpl.msc or PowerShell to review and update ownership accordingly.
